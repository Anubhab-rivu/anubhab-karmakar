Metrology of Screw Threads

Understanding the measurement, inspection, and quality control of screw thread profiles used in mechanical assemblies.

5.1

Terminology of Screw Thread

Before we measure anything, we need to know the language of screw threads. Just like you cannot describe a person without knowing the words for hands, legs, and face — you cannot describe a screw thread without knowing its technical terms. Each term refers to a specific geometric feature that affects how two mating threads fit together.

Fig. 5.1 — Cross-sectional view of an external (male) screw thread showing key terminology

5.1.1 Major Diameter (d or D)

Major Diameter Also called: Nominal Diameter / Outside Diameter

The major diameter is the largest diameter of a screw thread. For an external (male) thread such as a bolt, it is the diameter of an imaginary cylinder that just touches the crests (peaks) of the thread. For an internal (female) thread such as a nut, it just touches the roots (valleys) of the thread.

Think of it this way — if you placed a ruler across the top of a bolt's thread (the highest points), the measurement you get is the major diameter. This is the number we refer to when we say "M10 bolt" or "¼-inch bolt." It's the face value of the screw.

Important Note

Major diameter is also called nominal diameter because it is the value used to identify and name the thread (e.g., M6, M8, M10 in metric, or 1/4", 3/8" in inch threads). In IS standards, it is denoted by lowercase d for external threads and uppercase D for internal threads.

How it is measured: Using a bench micrometer or a floating carriage measuring machine with a plain (smooth) setting cylinder as a reference standard. The micrometer anvils rest on the crests of the thread.

5.1.2 Minor Diameter (d₁ or D₁)

Minor Diameter Also called: Core Diameter / Root Diameter

The minor diameter is the smallest diameter of a screw thread. For an external thread, it is the diameter of the imaginary cylinder that just touches the roots (bottom of the grooves) of the thread. For an internal thread, it just touches the crests of the thread.

Imagine measuring the bolt at its thinnest part — right at the bottom of the grooves. That measurement is the minor diameter. In old texts, it was called the "core diameter" because it represents the solid metallic core of the bolt, which carries the tensile load.

Why Minor Diameter Matters

The minor diameter determines the tensile stress area of the bolt — a smaller minor diameter means a weaker bolt. Engineers calculate the strength of threaded fasteners based on the minor diameter. It is critical in design against bolt failure.

How it is measured: Using a floating carriage micrometer with small V-shaped pieces (prisms) that seat into the roots of the thread. The depth of the root is thus captured accurately.

5.1.3 Effective Diameter / Pitch Diameter (d₂ or D₂)

Effective Diameter (Pitch Diameter) Most Critical Dimension

The effective diameter (also called pitch diameter) is the diameter of an imaginary cylinder that passes through the thread profile at such points where the width of the thread ridge equals the width of the groove. At this cylinder, the metal portion and the air gap are equal.

This is the most important dimension in screw thread measurement. Why? Because the effective diameter is what actually controls the fit between mating threads. If the effective diameter of a bolt and nut are correctly matched, they will assemble smoothly and lock together with the right amount of grip — not too loose, not too tight.

Critical Point

The effective diameter cannot be directly measured with a simple instrument. It must be calculated using measurements taken over wires (two-wire or three-wire method) placed in the thread grooves. This is why the wire methods are so important in Thread Metrology.

Relationship between diameters (ISO Metric Thread)
d₂ = d − 0.6495 × p
d₁ = d − 1.2990 × p

Where: d = major diameter, p = pitch, d₁ = minor diameter, d₂ = effective diameter

5.1.4 Pitch (p)

Pitch Linear distance between thread crests

Pitch is the axial distance (measured parallel to the thread axis) between a point on one thread and the corresponding point on the next adjacent thread. In simple terms, it is the distance from one crest to the next crest, or from one root to the next root.

Pitch tells us how fine or coarse the thread is. A thread with a small pitch (e.g., 0.5 mm) is a fine thread — it has many threads per unit length. A thread with a large pitch (e.g., 2.0 mm) is a coarse thread — it advances more per revolution.

Coarse Threads

Larger pitch. Faster assembly. Better in soft materials. More resistant to stripping. Used in general-purpose fastening (nuts, bolts).

Fine Threads

Smaller pitch. Better vibrational resistance. Higher tensile strength. Used in precision applications, aircraft fittings, and where fine adjustment is needed.

Pitch vs. Lead

Pitch: distance between adjacent threads (always measured in mm or inches).
Lead: the distance a screw advances axially in one complete revolution.
For a single-start thread: Lead = Pitch.
For a double-start thread: Lead = 2 × Pitch.
For an n-start thread: Lead = n × Pitch.

How it is measured: Using a screw pitch gauge (a set of blades with teeth of known pitch), pitch measuring machine, or optically using a toolmaker's microscope.

5.1.5 Thread Angle (2α)

Thread Angle (Included Angle) Also: Flank Angle (α = half-angle)

The thread angle (also called the included angle or angle of thread) is the angle between the two flanks of the thread, measured in the axial plane (the plane containing the axis of the screw). Each half of this angle is called the flank angle (α).

The flanks are the sloping sides of the thread. The angle between them is what makes threads from different standards incompatible — an ISO metric thread at 60° will not properly mate with a Whitworth thread at 55°, even if the pitch and diameter happen to match.

Thread System Thread Angle (2α) Flank Angle (α) Application
ISO Metric (M) 60° 30° General engineering worldwide
Unified National (UN) 60° 30° US, UK, Canada fasteners
British Standard Whitworth (BSW) 55° 27.5° Older British machinery
ACME Thread 29° 14.5° Power transmission (lead screws)
Square Thread 0° 0° Maximum power transmission

How it is measured: Using a toolmaker's microscope or optical profile projector, by projecting a magnified shadow of the thread cross-section onto a screen and comparing it against templates.

5.1.6 Other Important Terminology

  • Crest: The top surface (peak) of the thread ridge — the highest point of an external thread or the lowest point of an internal thread.
  • Root: The bottom surface (valley) of the thread groove — opposite of crest.
  • Flank: The straight inclined surface connecting the crest and the root. The two flanks of a thread form a "V" shape.
  • Helix Angle: The angle that the helix of the thread makes with a plane perpendicular to the thread axis. A steeper helix angle means faster advancement per revolution.
  • Addendum: The radial distance between the crest and the pitch (effective) cylinder — measured outward from the pitch line to the crest.
  • Dedendum: The radial distance between the pitch (effective) cylinder and the root — measured inward from the pitch line to the root.
  • Thread Form: The cross-sectional shape of the thread cut by a plane containing the axis (e.g., V-form, square, trapezoidal, ACME).
  • Thread Series: Threads of the same form that have a specific combination of diameter and pitch (e.g., M10×1.5 — 10 mm diameter, 1.5 mm pitch).
5.2

Working Principle and Use of Measuring Instruments

Now that we know the terms, let us understand the tools and methods used to actually measure these dimensions on real screw thread components. Three techniques are covered in this unit:

  • Floating Carriage Micrometer — a precision machine for measuring all three diameters (major, minor, and effective).
  • Screw Thread Micrometer — a handheld instrument with special anvils for quick effective diameter measurement.
  • Two-Wire Method — a calculated method for accurately determining the effective (pitch) diameter using precision wires.

5.2.1 Floating Carriage Micrometer (Bench Micrometer)

The floating carriage micrometer is a laboratory-grade precision instrument designed specifically for the measurement of threaded components such as thread plug gauges, ring gauges, and cylindrical shafts. Unlike a handheld micrometer, this machine is mounted on a bench and uses a differential (comparative) measurement technique.

Why "Floating Carriage"?

The micrometer head and anvil assembly is mounted on a carriage that can slide freely (i.e., "float") perpendicular to the axis of the workpiece. This ensures that the measuring faces always make proper contact with the thread flanks at right angles, eliminating errors due to misalignment.

Construction & Main Parts

The instrument has three main units arranged on a precision base casting:

  1. 1
    Base Casting with Centres: A pair of hardened steel centres are mounted on the base on two precision brackets. The workpiece (screw thread or plug gauge) is mounted between these centres, exactly like on a lathe, ensuring the thread is held along its true axis.
  2. 2
    Fixed Carriage: This carriage supports the two centres. It does not move during measurement. The centres are adjustable so different workpiece lengths can be accommodated.
  3. 3
    Floating Carriage: A second carriage is mounted at exactly 90° to the fixed carriage (i.e., perpendicular to the thread axis). On this carriage, the micrometer head assembly is mounted on one side and a fixed spring-loaded anvil on the other side.
  4. 4
    Micrometer Head: Has a large thimble with a vernier scale, enabling readings to an accuracy of 0.001 mm or 0.002 mm. The thimble is rotated to advance the spindle toward the workpiece.
  5. 5
    Fiducial Indicator: A small dial indicator (or spirit level type indicator) mounted on the fixed anvil side. Its purpose is to ensure all measurements are taken at the same measuring pressure — this is critical for consistent comparative readings. When the needle of the fiducial indicator is at its reference position, the reading is valid.

Fig. 5.2 — Schematic layout of the floating carriage micrometer showing centres, workpiece, carriage, and measuring head

Procedure for Measuring Major Diameter

  1. 1
    Mount the threaded workpiece between the two centres of the machine. Ensure it is properly aligned along the axis.
  2. 2
    Select a calibrated setting cylinder (a precision-ground cylinder) whose diameter is close to the major diameter of the thread to be measured. This is the reference standard.
  3. 3
    Place the setting cylinder between the anvil and micrometer spindle. Advance the spindle until the fiducial indicator shows the reference zero position. Note the reading R₁ (reference).
  4. 4
    Remove the cylinder and replace with the threaded workpiece. Advance the spindle to touch the crests of the thread. When the fiducial indicator returns to the reference position, note the reading R₂ (workpiece).
  5. 5
    Calculate: Major Diameter = Diameter of Setting Cylinder ± (R₂ − R₁)
Formula — Major Diameter
D_major = D_std ± ΔR
where ΔR = R₂ − R₁ (difference in micrometer readings)
D_std = diameter of the setting cylinder (known value)

Measuring Minor Diameter

For minor diameter, the plain spindle and anvil are replaced with small V-shaped prisms (called V-pieces or small vees). These prisms seat themselves neatly into the roots of the thread on either side of the workpiece, and the micrometer reading then gives a dimension related to the minor diameter. The V-piece geometry must be accounted for in the final calculation. The procedure is otherwise identical to the major diameter measurement — using a setting cylinder of known diameter as the standard.

Advantage of Floating Carriage Micrometer

Since the carriage is free to float (move perpendicular to the workpiece axis), the measurement is always taken in the true diametral plane regardless of any slight eccentricity in mounting. The fiducial indicator also ensures that all readings are taken at identical contact pressure, eliminating elastic deformation errors.

5.2.2 Screw Thread Micrometer

The screw thread micrometer is a modified version of the standard outside micrometer, specially designed to measure the effective (pitch) diameter of external threads. It is a handheld, shop-floor instrument suitable for quick, direct readings without the need for complex calculations.

Key Feature — Special Anvils

Unlike a standard micrometer with flat anvils, the screw thread micrometer has a conical (cone-shaped) spindle and a V-shaped (double-cone) anvil. These specially shaped anvils seat themselves into the thread flanks at the pitch line, effectively measuring the effective diameter directly.

Construction

  • Frame and Barrel: Same as a standard micrometer — a C-shaped steel frame with a thimble that rotates over a graduated barrel. Reads to 0.01 mm (or 0.001 mm on a precision version).
  • Cone-shaped Spindle (Movable Anvil): The spindle end is ground to a cone whose included angle matches the thread angle (60° for metric threads). This cone seats inside one side of the thread groove, touching both flanks simultaneously.
  • V-shaped Fixed Anvil (Double Cone): The fixed anvil is ground with a V-groove on its face. The V is shaped to engage the thread on the opposite side — it straddles the thread crest and touches both flanks.
  • Interchangeable Anvil Sets: Since thread angle and pitch vary across thread systems, the anvil sets are interchangeable. A set of anvils is provided for different pitches (e.g., 0.5 mm, 0.75 mm, 1.0 mm, 1.25 mm, 1.5 mm, 1.75 mm, 2.0 mm for metric threads).

Fig. 5.3 — Screw thread micrometer with cone spindle and V-anvil engaging thread flanks at the pitch line

Procedure for Use

  1. 1
    Select the correct set of anvils for the pitch of the thread to be measured (e.g., for an M10×1.5 thread, select the 1.5 mm pitch anvil set).
  2. 2
    Check the zero setting by closing the anvils together and verifying the reading is zero. If not, adjust using the thimble lock or the barrel sleeve.
  3. 3
    Place the screw thread between the anvils. The V-anvil goes on one side of the thread and the cone spindle on the opposite side.
  4. 4
    Rotate the thimble slowly until the anvils make contact with the thread flanks. Do not force — apply only gentle pressure.
  5. 5
    Read the value directly from the barrel and thimble scales. This reading is the effective (pitch) diameter of the thread.

Advantages

✓ Quick and direct reading
✓ No calculation needed
✓ Portable (handheld)
✓ Suitable for shop-floor use
✓ Relatively low cost

Limitations

✗ Only measures external threads
✗ Requires separate anvil set per pitch
✗ Less accurate than wire method
✗ Does not detect pitch error
✗ Operator skill affects result

5.2.3 Two-Wire Method for Effective Diameter Measurement

The two-wire method is one of the most widely used techniques for the accurate measurement of effective (pitch) diameter of screw threads. It is based on the principle of placing precision-ground cylindrical wires into the thread grooves and then measuring the distance over the wires using a micrometer.

Why use wires?

The effective diameter cannot be directly contacted with flat anvils. Precision wires, being round, naturally seat themselves into the thread groove and contact the flanks at a specific point. By knowing the exact wire diameter and the geometry of the thread, we can mathematically work back to find the effective diameter.

Concept of Best-Size Wire

Not just any wire will give the most accurate result. The wire size that touches the thread flanks exactly at the pitch line (i.e., at the effective diameter cylinder) is called the Best-Size Wire. Using best-size wires is recommended because errors in the thread angle have minimum effect on the measurement.

Best-Size Wire Diameter Formula
d_best = (P/2) × sec(α)

For ISO Metric & Unified threads (α = 30°):
d_best = 0.5774 × P (i.e., P/√3)

For Whitworth threads (α = 27.5°):
d_best = 0.5637 × P

Where: P = pitch, α = flank angle (half of thread angle)

Fig. 5.4 — Two precision wires placed in alternate grooves on opposite sides; micrometer measures across outside of both wires (dimension M)

Step-by-Step Procedure

  1. 1
    Select the wires: Choose two steel wires of identical, precisely known diameter. Ideally, use the best-size wire diameter for the given pitch and thread angle.
  2. 2
    Mount the workpiece: Place the threaded workpiece (bolt, plug gauge, etc.) between the centres of the floating carriage micrometer. Alternatively, hold it in V-blocks.
  3. 3
    Take standard reading: Place a setting cylinder (of known diameter) between the micrometer anvils. Note the reading R_std.
  4. 4
    Place wires in grooves: Place one wire in a groove on the left side and the second wire in a groove on the right side (in diametrically opposite positions) of the workpiece.
  5. 5
    Take measurement reading: Bring the micrometer anvils against the two wires (not the thread crests). Note the reading R_work.
  6. 6
    Calculate M (dimension over wires): M = D_std ± (R_work − R_std), where D_std is the diameter of the setting cylinder.
  7. 7
    Calculate effective diameter: Apply the formula below.
Effective Diameter — Two-Wire Method
E = T + P_constant

Where:
T = M − 2d (dimension under the wires)
M = measured dimension over the wires
d = diameter of each wire
P_constant = correction factor depending on pitch, wire diameter, and thread angle

For ISO Metric threads (60°): P_constant = 0.86603 × p
For Whitworth threads (55°): P_constant = 0.96049 × p

Therefore: E = M − 2d + 0.86603p (for ISO Metric, using best-size wire)
Two-Wire vs. Three-Wire Method

The two-wire method uses wires on opposite sides of the thread — one wire on the left side, one on the right. The measurement M is taken over both. This method is commonly used with a bench micrometer/floating carriage machine and relies on comparative measurement against a setting standard.

The three-wire method uses two wires on one side and one wire on the other, making it self-centering. It is considered slightly more accurate and can be used with a simple handheld micrometer. For both methods, the effective diameter formula is the same — but the P constant values may differ slightly based on the computation approach used.

5.3

Construction and Use of Thread Gauges

In mass production, it is not practical (or economical) to measure every single threaded component with a micrometer and calculate its effective diameter. Instead, we use thread gauges — precision reference tools that quickly determine whether a thread is within its acceptable dimensional limits. Gauges do not give a numerical reading; they give a simple pass/fail decision.

Purpose of Thread Gauges

Thread gauges are used in production inspection to ensure that manufactured threads conform to specified tolerance limits. They work on the Taylor's Principle (GO-NO GO principle) and are particularly useful for high-volume production where speed of inspection is critical.

Thread Plug Gauge
GO end NO-GO end

Checks internal threads (nuts, tapped holes). The gauge is inserted into the threaded hole.

For: Verifying that the nut / threaded hole is correctly sized.

Thread Ring Gauge
GO end NO-GO end

Checks external threads (bolts, studs). The gauge is fitted over the threaded workpiece.

For: Verifying that the bolt / stud is correctly sized.

Thread Snap Gauge
GO anvils NO-GO anvils

Checks external threads faster than a ring gauge. The screw slides between two sets of anvils.

For: High-speed, mass production inspection of external threads.

5.3.0 The GO / NO-GO Gauging Principle (Taylor's Principle)

All thread gauges work on Taylor's Principle of gauging. This principle states:

Taylor's Principle of Gauging

GO gauge: Should check all dimensions simultaneously (i.e., it must be a full-form gauge that engages the complete thread profile). If it passes through/onto the workpiece, the part is within the maximum material limit.

NO-GO gauge: Should check only one dimension at a time. If it does NOT pass (i.e., it is stopped by the workpiece), the part is within the minimum material limit. If it does pass, the part is too small/large and is rejected.

A part is acceptable (passes inspection) only if:
→ The GO gauge passes (enters/fits)
→ The NO-GO gauge does not pass (is stopped/rejected)

Acceptance Condition
Part ACCEPTED if: GO gauge → PASSES (enters) AND NO-GO gauge → FAILS (does not enter)
Part REJECTED if: GO gauge fails OR NO-GO gauge passes

5.3.1 Thread Plug Gauge

A thread plug gauge is used to inspect internal threads — i.e., nuts and threaded holes (tapped holes). The gauge is a precision-machined cylindrical plug with external threads on both ends (or on both halves of a double-ended gauge). It is screwed into the threaded hole to check whether the hole's thread is correct.

Construction

  • Material: Made from high-carbon steel or alloy tool steel, hardened and precision-ground to extremely close tolerances (typically IT3 to IT5 quality). Ceramic materials are also used for high-wear applications.
  • GO End (Full-Form Gauge): The GO end is a full-form thread — it has all the thread features (complete profile: flanks, crest, root) at the correct pitch. It has a longer length (usually 6–10 threads) to ensure all thread elements are checked simultaneously. The GO end is made to the maximum material limit (i.e., the smallest acceptable internal thread size).
  • NO-GO End (Truncated Form): The NO-GO end is shorter (usually 2–3 threads) and may have a truncated (partially cut) profile — it only checks the effective diameter, not all elements. It is made to the minimum material limit (largest acceptable effective diameter). The shorter thread length ensures it can only enter if the effective diameter is too large.
  • Handle: The two gauge ends are mounted on opposite ends of a steel or aluminium handle for easy gripping. For large gauge sizes, the GO and NO-GO may be separate gauges (individual handles).
  • Identification: The GO end is usually marked or knurled differently from the NO-GO end to prevent mix-up during inspection.

Fig. 5.5 — Double-ended thread plug gauge showing the longer GO end (full-form) and shorter NO-GO end (truncated)

How to Use — Thread Plug Gauge

  1. 1
    Clean the threaded hole and the gauge thoroughly (no chips, coolant, or burrs). Even a small chip can jam the gauge and cause false rejection.
  2. 2
    Insert the GO end into the threaded hole. It should screw in freely, without binding or force, through the entire threaded length. If it enters smoothly → Thread is acceptable from the GO side.
  3. 3
    Now try the NO-GO end. It should not enter the thread — it should stop after at most 1–2 turns due to interference. If it does not enter → Thread is acceptable from the NO-GO side.
  4. 4
    Part is ACCEPTED if GO enters freely AND NO-GO is rejected. Part is REJECTED if GO does not enter (under-sized / damaged thread) OR NO-GO enters freely (oversized thread).
Important Handling Rule

Never force the GO gauge into the hole. If it does not enter easily with finger pressure, the thread is under-tolerance — forcing will damage the precision gauge. Thread plug gauges are hardened to very high accuracy and must be stored carefully in cases with protective caps when not in use.

5.3.2 Thread Ring Gauge

A thread ring gauge is used to inspect external threads — bolts, studs, and threaded shafts. It is a ring-shaped gauge with internal threads of known, precision dimensions. The threaded component is passed through the ring to check its external thread.

Construction

  • Material: Hardened and precision-ground alloy tool steel. The internal thread is lapped to extremely fine surface finish and accurate dimensions.
  • GO Ring Gauge: A solid ring with full-form internal threads. It is made to the maximum material limit of the external thread (i.e., the largest acceptable bolt size). The GO ring must slide freely over the entire length of the threaded portion. It typically has a smooth outer surface or is identified by a groove or colour mark.
  • NO-GO Ring Gauge: Also a solid ring but with a shorter, truncated thread profile. Made to the minimum material limit. It should NOT be able to go over the threaded bolt if the bolt is within tolerance. It is usually identified by an annular groove on its outer face or by a different colour band.
  • Adjustable Ring Gauge: A variation that has a slit through the ring body plus an adjusting screw, allowing the internal diameter to be fine-tuned using a setting plug (master plug). This type can be recalibrated as the thread wears, making it more economical. It is common in production environments.

Fig. 5.6 — Thread ring gauges (GO and NO-GO) used to inspect external threads. Note the annular groove on the NO-GO ring for identification.

How to Use — Thread Ring Gauge

  1. 1
    Clean the bolt and the gauge rings thoroughly. Check that neither has any burrs or chips.
  2. 2
    Engage the GO ring gauge at the threaded end of the bolt. It should screw onto the bolt easily, all the way to the shoulder, with gentle hand rotation only. No forcing allowed.
  3. 3
    Now try the NO-GO ring gauge. It should not be able to be screwed onto the bolt (beyond 1–2 partial turns). Resistance indicates the bolt's effective diameter is within maximum limit.
  4. 4
    Accept if GO passes, NO-GO fails. Reject if GO does not pass (thread too small/damaged) or NO-GO passes fully (thread too large / undercut).
Solid vs. Adjustable Ring Gauge

Solid ring gauges are used for inspection of finished parts — they are highly accurate but cannot be adjusted when worn. Adjustable ring gauges can be re-set using a setting plug to compensate for wear, making them more economical in high-volume production. However, they must be checked and adjusted regularly to maintain accuracy.

5.3.3 Thread Snap Gauge (Thread Caliper Gauge)

A thread snap gauge (also called a thread caliper gauge) is another type of gauge for checking external threads. Unlike a ring gauge (which the bolt must be screwed through), the snap gauge allows the threaded part to simply be passed between fixed anvils or rollers. This makes it much faster for high-volume production inspection.

Construction

  • Frame: A rigid C-shaped or U-shaped steel frame (similar to a snap gauge for plain diameters) that provides the structural backbone of the instrument.
  • GO Anvils/Rollers: Two hardened anvils (or precision rollers) set at the GO gauging limit. They are shaped to engage the thread flanks and check the effective diameter. Roller-type GO elements allow the threaded component to simply be slid (dropped) through by gravity for ultra-fast inspection.
  • NO-GO Anvils: A second pair of anvils set slightly closer together (at the NO-GO limit), which the bolt should not pass through if within tolerance.
  • Adjustability: Most production snap gauges are adjustable using set screws or precision shims, allowing them to be set to exact dimensions using setting masters. This also allows for wear compensation.
  • Handle: An ergonomic handle is provided for comfortable operation at high throughput speeds.

Fig. 5.7 — Thread snap gauge with GO and NO-GO anvil pairs. The bolt slides through without rotating, enabling rapid inspection.

How to Use — Thread Snap Gauge

  1. 1
    Set the snap gauge to the correct GO and NO-GO limits using a setting master (a reference bolt of known dimensions).
  2. 2
    Present the threaded component to the GO gauging station. The part should pass through the GO anvils easily (or drop through by gravity for roller-type gauges).
  3. 3
    Present the same component to the NO-GO gauging station. The part should not pass through the NO-GO anvils (they should stop the part).
  4. 4
    Part is accepted if it passes GO and is stopped by NO-GO. Reject otherwise.

Snap vs. Ring Gauge

Ring gauge: bolt must be screwed through → slower, more thorough (full thread engagement).

Snap gauge: bolt just slides through → much faster, but checks fewer thread elements simultaneously.

Where Snap Gauges Are Used

Ideal for high-volume mass production lines where inspection speed is critical — automotive fasteners, screw manufacturing, and wherever thousands of parts need checking per hour.
